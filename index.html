DIVE INTO THE ABYSS

EPIPELAGIC de 0 à 200

This is the only layer where enough sunlight penetrates for photosynthesis. Because of this, it is home to nearly all marine plants (phytoplankton) and the vast majority of ocean life, including sharks, whales, and coral reefs.
Temperature is the highest of all zones, varying by latitude.

MESOPELAGIC (660 ft/200 m)

Light becomes very faint here.
No plants can grow, but many animals (like squid and hatchetfish) have evolved large eyes to see in the dim light.
This is also where bioluminescence begins to appear as creatures create their own light to hunt or hide.
Temperature drop rapidly in this layer (the thermocline).

BATHYPELAGIC (3300 ft/1000 m)

It is pitch black.
The only light comes from bioluminescent organisms.
The water pressure is immense, reaching up to 5,800 pounds per square inch.
Animals here, like the anglerfish, often have slow metabolisms and wait for "marine snow" (organic debris) to fall from above.

ABYSSOPELAGIC (13,000 ft/4000 m)

The name comes from the Greek word for "bottomless."
The water is near freezing, and the pressure is high enough to crush most organisms.
It is a world of silent, dark plains.
Despite the harshness, invertebrates like sea baskets and tiny squids survive here.

HADALPELAGIC (13,000 ft/ )

Named after Hades, the Greek god of the underworld.
This zone consists of the deepest V-shaped trenches in the ocean floor.
Life is sparse but exists mostly amphipods (shrimp-like creatures) and specialized snailfish that can withstand pressure equivalent to an elephant standing on a thumb.
